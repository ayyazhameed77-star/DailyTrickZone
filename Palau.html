Palau Travel Guide

The Underwater Wonder of Micronesia

🇵🇼 Country Overview

Republic of Palau

Capital: Ngerulmud

Population: ~18,000

Languages: Palauan, English

Currency: United States Dollar (USD)

Best Time to Visit: November to May (Dry Season)

Palau is a breathtaking island nation located in the western Pacific Ocean, part of the Micronesia region, approximately 800 kilometers east of the Philippines. Comprising over 340 islands, this tropical paradise covers just 459 square kilometers of land but spans across an exclusive economic zone of nearly 630,000 square kilometers. Palau is renowned worldwide as one of the planet's premier diving destinations, home to an extraordinary diversity of marine life and some of the most pristine coral reef ecosystems on Earth.

The nation gained independence on October 1, 1994, following a Compact of Free Association with the United States. Palau's history stretches back over 3,000 years, with indigenous settlers arriving from Southeast Asia. The islands were first visited by Europeans in the 16th century and were later controlled by Spain, Germany, Japan, and the United States before achieving sovereignty. In 2015, Palau created the Palau National Marine Sanctuary, protecting 80% of its territorial waters — one of the largest marine protected areas in the world, demonstrating the nation's profound commitment to ocean conservation.

🏙️ Famous Cities & Towns

Koror

The former capital and largest city of Palau, serving as the economic and commercial hub. Koror is home to approximately two-thirds of the country's population and offers a range of accommodations, restaurants, and dive shops. The city features the Belau National Museum, the Etpison Museum, and serves as the gateway to the world-famous Rock Islands. The bustling Koror State offers vibrant markets and stunning waterfront views.

Ngerulmud

The current capital of Palau, located on the island of Babeldaob, the largest island in Micronesia. Ngerulmud is one of the world's smallest capitals by population, featuring the impressive Capitol Complex with its distinctive traditional Palauan architecture. The area offers a peaceful atmosphere surrounded by lush tropical rainforest and is a testament to Palau's unique blend of modern governance and traditional culture.

Melekeok

A traditional village on Babeldaob Island, Melekeok offers visitors an authentic glimpse into Palauan village life. The village is known for its beautiful stone pathways, traditional bai (meeting houses) adorned with intricate storyboards, and its proximity to stunning natural attractions. It provides a serene escape from the busier areas of Koror while maintaining deep cultural roots.

Peleliu

A small island south of Koror, Peleliu is famous for its significant World War II history and extraordinary diving sites. The island was the site of one of the Pacific War's bloodiest battles, and remnants of tanks, artillery, and bunkers can still be explored today. Beneath the waves, Peleliu offers incredible drift diving through pristine coral walls teeming with sharks, rays, and vibrant marine life.

🏝️ Most Beautiful Places

Palau is a paradise of extraordinary natural beauty both above and below the water. The Rock Islands Southern Lagoon, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is the crown jewel — a collection of over 400 limestone islands covered in lush vegetation, rising dramatically from turquoise waters like emerald mushrooms. These ancient coral reefs uplifted millions of years ago create a maze of hidden lagoons, secret beaches, and marine lakes, including the world-famous Jellyfish Lake, where millions of harmless golden jellyfish have evolved without stingers, creating a surreal swimming experience.

Below the surface, Palau offers some of the most spectacular diving on Earth. Blue Corner is legendary among divers for its sheer wall dives, strong currents, and incredible shark encounters. The German Channel, blasted through the reef during the colonial era, attracts manta rays by the dozen. For snorkelers, the Milky Way is a unique mud bath in a secluded lagoon, where white limestone mud is said to have rejuvenating properties. On land, the Ngardmau Waterfall, the tallest in Micronesia, cascades through pristine jungle, while the stone monoliths of Badrulchau offer mysterious ancient ruins overlooking the ocean. Kayangel Atoll in the north provides untouched paradise with pristine beaches and vibrant coral gardens.

🌺 Culture & Travel Tips

Palauan culture is a rich tapestry woven from ancient Micronesian traditions, colonial influences, and modern Pacific identity. The traditional social structure revolves around matrilineal clans, with women holding significant authority in land ownership and family matters. The bai, or traditional meeting house, remains the center of village life — these beautifully carved wooden structures feature storyboards depicting Palauan legends and history. Respect for elders and community leaders is paramount, and visitors should always seek permission before entering villages or taking photographs of people.

Palauan cuisine reflects the bounty of both land and sea, with taro, cassava, and coconut forming staples alongside fresh fish, shellfish, and fruit bat soup — a local delicacy. The traditional money system using rai stones, massive limestone discs up to 4 meters in diameter, continues to hold cultural significance despite the use of modern currency for daily transactions. When visiting, dress modestly away from resort areas, bring reef-safe sunscreen to protect the fragile marine ecosystem, and never touch or stand on coral while snorkeling or diving.

Essential Tips: Book dive trips well in advance as Palau's sites are world-renowned, bring a waterproof camera for unforgettable underwater photography, carry cash as credit card acceptance is limited outside major hotels, and respect the Rock Islands permit system designed to protect this fragile paradise. Learn a few Palauan phrases like "Alii" (hello) and "Sulang" (thank you) — the locals will appreciate your effort. Most importantly, embrace the Palauan philosophy of "Buik" — living in harmony with nature — and you'll discover why Jacques Cousteau called Palau's waters one of the seven underwater wonders of the world.
