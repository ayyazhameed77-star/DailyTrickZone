Discover Paraguay

Paraguay is South America's best-kept secret—a landlocked gem of roughly 7 million people nestled right in the center of the continent. Unlike its famous neighbors, Paraguay does not shout for attention; it simply waits, warm and welcoming, for curious travelers to arrive. The country has two official languages: Spanish and Guaraní—the latter spoken by nearly 90% of the population, making it the most widely spoken indigenous language on the continent. The currency is the Paraguayan Guaraní, and the flag bears red, white, and blue stripes symbolizing courage, peace, and liberty. From the mighty Paraná and Paraguay rivers to the red-earth ruins of Jesuit missions, this is a nation where ancient traditions and modern life flow together as naturally as the currents of its rivers.

Asunción - The Hottest Capital with the Coolest Soul

Asunción is considered the hottest capital in the Americas, and I am not just talking about the temperature. Founded in 1537 along the eastern bank of the Paraguay River, this city of 3.5 million people pulses with a laid-back rhythm that feels almost rebellious in our hyper-connected world. The historic downtown is a fascinating patchwork of colonial mansions, weathered 20th-century façades, and sudden bursts of modern street art. The Palacio de los López, the pink presidential palace, glows ethereally at night and anchors the waterfront Costanera, where locals gather to sip tereré and watch the sun bleed into the river. Condé Nast Traveler recently named Asunción one of the best places to visit in 2026, and it is easy to see why: a new wave of chefs is reinventing Paraguayan cuisine, specialty coffee shops are blooming, and restored heritage buildings now house boutique hotels and creative spaces. Do not miss the Panteón Nacional de los Héroes, a small but stunning mausoleum where an eternal flame honors the country's fallen. Asunción is not polished, and that is precisely its charm—it is real, raw, and refreshingly human.

Encarnación - Where Carnival Meets Centuries

On the banks of the Paraná River, just across from Argentina, lies Encarnación—a city that effortlessly blends beach-town relaxation with whispers of history. The Costanera waterfront comes alive each evening as families stroll, couples share mate, and the lights of Posadas flicker on the opposite shore. San José Beach offers a surprising slice of riverfront paradise in a landlocked country, with calm, shallow waters perfect for a refreshing dip. But Encarnación's true soul lies just outside the city: the Jesuit Missions of La Santísima Trinidad de Paraná and Jesús de Tavarangue. These UNESCO World Heritage ruins, built in the 17th century, are masterpieces of red stone and faith, where indigenous Guaraní and European Jesuits once created a unique utopian society. Wander the silent arches and bell towers as the sun sets, and you will feel centuries collapse into a single breath. If you visit in January or February, the Carnaval Encarnaceno transforms the city into a riot of feathers, drums, and dancing—often called the most authentic carnival in South America after Rio. It is affordable, joyful, and gloriously untouristy.

Ciudad del Este - The City That Powers a Continent

Ciudad del Este sits at the triple border of Paraguay, Brazil, and Argentina, and it is unlike anywhere else on Earth. By day, it is a shopper's fever dream—thousands of Brazilians and Argentinians flood across the Friendship Bridge to hunt for electronics, perfumes, and bargains in a sprawling maze of malls and street stalls. But look beyond the commerce, and you will find one of the modern world's greatest wonders: the Itaipú Dam. This colossal hydroelectric plant, shared with Brazil, generates enough electricity to power nearly all of Paraguay and a fifth of Brazil. Standing before the spillways as water thunders into the Paraná River is a humbling reminder of human ambition. The dam offers free guided tours, a museum of Guaraní land and culture, and an evening light show projected onto the reservoir walls. Ciudad del Este is chaotic, loud, and unapologetically commercial—but it is also the gateway to the majestic Iguazú Falls, just minutes away in Brazil and Argentina. Love it or hate it, this city will leave an impression.

San Bernardino - The Summer Soul of Paraguay

Just an hour from Asunción, San Bernardino feels like stepping into a different world—a world of pine-scented hills, German-influenced architecture, and the vast, shimmering expanse of Lake Ypacaraí. Founded by German immigrants in the late 19th century, this lakeside town has become Paraguay's most beloved summer retreat, where Asuncenos escape the capital's sweltering heat on weekends. The waterfront buzzes with sailboats, kayaks, and families grilling sopa paraguaya by the shore. The town's European heritage is still visible in its chalet-style homes, artisan breweries, and the occasional polka melody drifting from a neighborhood gathering. San Bernardino is not about ticking off tourist sights; it is about slowing down. Rent a paddleboat, eat fresh fried fish at a lakeside shack, and watch the sky turn violet over the water. If you want to understand how Paraguayans truly live, laugh, and recharge, come here on a Sunday afternoon when the tereré flows freely and the whole town feels like one big family reunion.

Travel Tips for Paraguay

  • ✈ Best time to visit: April to September for milder temperatures; January-February for carnival season.
  • 💰 Currency: Paraguayan Guaraní (PYG). Cash is essential in small towns; USD are accepted in some places.
  • 🧉 Don't miss: Try tereré (iced herbal mate), chipa cheese bread, sopa paraguaya, and local beef.
  • 🌎 Language: Spanish and Guaraní are both official. Most people speak both, and Guaraní words flavor everyday conversation.

Paraguay does not beg to be noticed. It simply opens its arms, offers you a gourd of tereré, and invites you to stay awhile. In a world obsessed with the next big thing, this quiet heart of South America reminds us that the greatest journeys are the ones we never planned. Jajei pora!
